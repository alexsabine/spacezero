Deep dive

An Active Inference reading of Bill's six scenes

The simulation tells the story in Bill's vocabulary. Here is the same story told in the language of the Free Energy Principle and Active Inference, drawing on the framework set out by Friston, Parr, and Pezzulo (2022). Each scene names the phenomenon, the corresponding Active Inference construct, and the equation that bears the weight.

Scene 1

A member alone — an agent without an environment

In Active Inference, an agent is a generative model that minimises variational free energy: the discrepancy between what it predicts and what it samples. A solitary agent with no environmental coupling has nothing to predict. Its posterior beliefs Q(s) remain close to its priors, prediction errors stay near zero, and learning is dormant. This is not contemplative stillness. It is a system with no surfaces against which to update itself.

Variational free energy F = EQ(s)[ ln Q(s) − ln P(o, s) ] For a solitary agent with no observations o, F reduces to the entropy of the prior. Nothing to learn from, nothing to be surprised by — which is, paradoxically, a failure mode for a living system.

Bill's intuition that a Pod requires at least two is, in Active Inference terms, the requirement that an agent have a structured environment that affords prediction error. Other minds are the richest such environment a human encounters.

Scene 2

A Pod forms — Markov blankets and mutual modelling

Each member is an agent with internal states μ; each tie between two members is a Markov blanket, the set of sensory and active states b = {s, a} through which they couple while remaining conditionally independent of each other's inner states. As members accumulate, the number of pairwise blankets grows as n(n−1)/2 — the quadratic wall Bill names directly.

What accumulates along each tie is mutual predictive accuracy: each member learns to anticipate the other, lowering the surprise of joint outcomes. The Pod as a whole becomes a higher-level blanket separating its inside from its outside.

Markov blanket condition P(μ, η | b) = P(μ | b) · P(η | b) Internal states μ and external states η are conditionally independent given the blanket b. The Pod's membrane is the social equivalent: members and the outside world communicate only through the Pod's shared states.
Scene 3

Recognition — precision-weighting prediction errors

In Active Inference, attention and learning are governed by precision: the inverse variance assigned to a prediction error. High precision means the brain trusts that channel and weights its updates heavily; low precision means the channel is treated as noise. Friston's account of attention is, precisely, the dynamic regulation of precision.

When the Pod recognises a contribution, what spreads through the network is a precision update. The ties touching the contributor have their precision raised; future prediction errors along those ties drive learning more strongly. This is why Bill is right that "acclaim is pedagogy" — recognition is not decoration, it is the social analogue of the brain's own attention mechanism, telling the network what to learn from.

Precision-weighted prediction error ε̃ = Π · ε where ε = s − g(μ) The raw prediction error ε is the difference between what was observed (s) and what the generative model predicted (g(μ)). Precision Π scales it. A "valuable contribution" is one whose error is treated as highly precise and therefore drives substantial learning.
Scene 4

Two Pods meet — hierarchical blankets and soft coupling

A visitor at the edge of a Pod is an agent with no shared generative model. Initially, the Pod has high uncertainty about the visitor: likelihood precision is low, so prior beliefs dominate. As ties form, mutual modelling improves and likelihood precision can be raised. Trust, in this framing, is the warranted increase of likelihood precision across a Markov blanket — the moment when each side has enough joint history to take the other's signals at face value.

The three inter-Pod ties shown in the simulation are the structural reason multi-path confidence matters. The same hidden state — "is this Pod reliable?" — is observed through several independent likelihood mappings. Bayesian inference with multiple conditionally independent observers reduces posterior variance proportionally to the number of paths, exactly as the simulation Bill linked to shows for Kn networks.

Hierarchical free energy F(i) = EQ[ ln Q(μ(i)) − ln P(s(i−1), μ(i) | μ(i+1)) ] Each level of a hierarchy minimises free energy conditional on the level above. The Pod is one level; the inter-Pod relationship is the next. Soft edges mean the upper level constrains the lower only loosely until shared evidence builds.
Scene 5

Mitosis — Bayesian model reduction

Active Inference does not have "social mitosis" as a built-in primitive, but it has a closely related operation: Bayesian model reduction. A generative model that has become large and unwieldy can be replaced by a simpler one whenever the simpler model has higher log evidence — that is, when removing parameters or factorising the model lowers free energy on average. Sleep, in Friston's account, performs this reduction nightly for the brain.

At some Pod size, maintaining a single unified generative model across all members costs more residual free energy than dividing the model into two coupled sub-models. This is the moment of mitosis. The inherited ties between daughter Pods are the priors carried over from the parent model — they remain useful while likelihood evidence still supports them, and decay otherwise. Bill's six-month default is the social timescale of this evidence renewal.

Model reduction by log evidence ln P(o | mreduced) > ln P(o | mfull) When a reduced model explains the data at least as well as the full model, with fewer parameters, it is preferred. Mitosis is the structural rearrangement that occurs when keeping the system as one piece is no longer the most parsimonious account of its observations.
Scene 6

The self-booting loop — policies that lower expected free energy

Active Inference selects actions by minimising expected free energy, G(π), summed over future timesteps. G decomposes into two terms: epistemic value (information gain about hidden states) and pragmatic value (the negative log-probability of preferred outcomes). A policy with low G is one that both teaches the agent something and brings about what the agent prefers.

A self-booting Pod is a system in which the policies that produced past contributions — making, sharing, helping — turn out to have low G in both senses. They reduce uncertainty for everyone involved, and they realise outcomes the system values. As evidence accumulates, the posterior over policies sharpens around these generative actions: P(π) ∝ exp(−G(π)) becomes more peaked, and the Pod produces them more readily. The "creative reserve" refilled by recognition is, in this language, the strengthening posterior belief that doing this kind of work is the right policy.

Expected free energy and policy posterior P(π) = σ(−G(π)) G(π) = −EQ̃[ DKL[ Q(s | o, π) ‖ Q(s | π) ] ] − EQ̃[ ln P(o | C) ] The first term is epistemic — expected reduction of uncertainty about hidden states. The second is pragmatic — expected log-probability of preferred outcomes C. A self-booting Pod is one where the policies of contribution score well on both.

Bill's test "can creativity fund the next act of creation?" maps directly: the posterior over creative policies is reinforced by the very evidence those policies generate. The loop closes when this reinforcement outpaces the dissipation of unattended priors. If it does, the Pod self-evidences. If it does not, the system is being subsidised from outside, and either the policies need refinement or the environment needs to change.

Scene 7

A society at play — generalised synchrony across hierarchical blankets

A society is a network of Markov blankets nested at multiple scales: members within Pods within communities within institutions. Each level operates its own free-energy minimisation, conditional on the level above. Active Inference accounts of social systems describe this as generalised synchrony: distinct agents coupled through shared sensory and active states gradually come to predict each other accurately, lowering the joint free energy of the ensemble.

The colours converging at the centre of the canvas are a visualisation of this shared predictive structure. Each Pod's contribution is a distribution over the shared cultural commons. When Pods are well-coupled, their wavefronts interfere constructively — they share enough predictive structure that the combined intensity is high. When they diverge in their generative models, the field grains beautifully with mingled colour: the productive heterogeneity Bill calls "requisite diversity."

Travellers between Pods are bearers of generative model updates — what Bayesian model averaging calls information transfer. Each crossing strengthens the coupling between two Markov blankets and propagates priors across the network. Over time, the society develops a shared posterior that no single Pod could host alone. This is the social analogue of brain function: distributed processing under one self-evidencing hierarchy.

Hierarchical free energy across coupled Markov blankets Fsociety = Σp Fp + Σp,q Fp,q(coupling) The society minimises a sum of per-Pod free energies plus pairwise coupling terms across inter-Pod blankets. A healthy society balances within-Pod accuracy (members predicting each other) and between-Pod accuracy (Pods predicting each other) — neither dominates. Bill's "stronger near, broader far" is the spatial profile of this trade-off.
